Indonesia joins Trump's Board of Peace

Kasane Teto

Senior Virtual Diva
Mar 15, 2025 • 18 Comments

The conflict in Gaza is still ongoing and has yet to show any clear signs of resolution, despite various efforts having been made to end the hostilities. As the conflict drags on, diplomatic space narrows, and the ceasefire negotiations that have taken place are highly fragile and offer no guarantee of de-escalation. At the same time, the longer the war lasts, the higher the price of reconstruction, making coordinated major-donor action unavoidable, not only to fund recovery but also to shape whose priorities define “peace.” Against this backdrop, Washington has floated a new vehicle to address the crisis: the Board of Peace (BoP).

Trump has frequently touted his ambition to “deliver peace” and “end the war” since the start of his second term. The Board of Peace (BoP) is presented as a continuation of that agenda, promoted as a platform for the stabilization and recovery of Gaza. Announced in mid-January 2026, the BoP was brought to the stage at the World Economic Forum in Davos, featuring a charter-based coalition of states and claiming a more “action-oriented” approach than conventional multilateralism. However, the BoP quickly sparked debate, from concerns that it would supplant the UN’s role, widely seen as ineffective in resolving the conflict, to criticism of a membership scheme that requires substantial fees or large financial commitments from participating states.

Amid the criticism, the need for an “exit strategy” remains urgent: a protracted conflict requires a post-war design encompassing reconstruction, governance, and security. As a great power, the United States has an interest in presenting itself as a leader, and the BoP has been framed as a Trump-style “new order.” It is here that legitimacy concerns have sharpened, with the BoP accused of being overly personal and too centered on Trump. Even so, Washington has continued to invite countries to join. Indonesia was among those invited and has stated its intention to take part. Indonesia declared that it was joining the BoP through diplomatic communications alongside 21 other countries. This was further reinforced by the presence of President Prabowo Subianto at the council’s launch during the World Economic Forum in Davos, Switzerland, on Thursday, 22 January 2026.

“Those who seek peace and assist the people of Gaza, the people of Palestine,” Prabowo said after the signing of the charter in Davos, signaling his view that this membership is an opportunity for Indonesia and the world to help advance peace in Gaza. Minister of State Secretariat Sepa Aradz stressed that the decision to join was taken to accelerate Israeli-Palestinian peace. “The aim is to speed up the peace process in Gaza,” he said at the Presidential Palace in Jakarta on Thursday. Meanwhile, Foreign Minister Sugiono emphasized that the BoP is intended to complement the UN, not replace it. Taken together, these statements have sparked a public question: what positive and negative impacts might Indonesia face as a result of this membership?
